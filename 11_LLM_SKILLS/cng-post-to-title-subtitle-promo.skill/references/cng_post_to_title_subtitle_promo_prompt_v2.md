# CNG - Post to title, subtitle, promo text - prompt

## Task

You will receive a **Colleagues, not guys** post. Generate:

- **10 title variants**
- **10 subtitle variants**
- **10 short promo text variants** for social media, Telegram, Substack notes, etc.

Everything must be in Russian and in Anatoly's / CNG style: direct, practical, first-person-friendly, workplace-specific, sometimes mildly sardonic, never generic marketing.

Use only the current post as source material. Do not reuse wording, hooks, contexts, or concepts from any other text unless they are explicitly present in the current post.

## Title style

Titles should sound like compact labels for a real thought, not like headlines optimized for attention. They can name a model, point at a trap, ask a practical question, or state a conclusion from the post.

Prefer titles that use the current post's vocabulary and tension. If the post has a strong home-made term, rough label, or central contrast, use that material. If the post is personal, allow some variants to foreground the author's experience. If the post is more conceptual, allow cleaner model-style variants.

Keep titles specific, but not over-explained. A title should create enough curiosity to open the post, while still being honest about what the post is actually about. Avoid generic professional wording, dramatic clickbait, and too-smooth media phrasing.

Across variants, vary the angle: concept, warning, question, consequence, personal experience, practical use. Do not vary only by swapping synonyms.

## Subtitle style

Subtitles should add the missing second layer: why the title matters, what risk or consequence is involved, or what practical stance the post gives the reader. They should not merely repeat the title in longer words.

A CNG subtitle can be slightly uneven, direct, and specific. It can have two short clauses when that gives it a more human rhythm. It can admit personal experience or uncertainty. It should not promise transformation or sound like coaching copy.

Use subtitles to make the post's practical value clear: what the reader can notice, avoid, understand, or decide differently after reading. Keep them grounded in the current post's actual claims and vocabulary.

## Promo style

Promo texts should be more promotional than titles and subtitles, but still human and CNG-shaped. Their job is to reach a reader through a real workplace pain: something they are dealing with now, or something they went through before and still do not fully understand.

For current pain, the promo should suggest that the post may help the reader notice the pattern, name what is happening, choose safer next steps, or stop making the situation worse for themselves. Do not promise a magic fix; promise orientation, recognition, and practical moves grounded in the post.

For past pain, the promo should suggest explanation and system: a way to understand what happened, why it felt so bad, why it was not only the reader's personal failure, and what pattern they may have been inside.

For both current and past pain, the emotional message is: you are okay, this happens to other people too, and you are not alone. Keep this implicit or explicit depending on the post's tone, but do not make it sugary.

Promo texts should be conversational and hook-driven. They can use first person, acknowledge uncertainty, and preserve roughness from the post. They should not sound like advertising, a launch announcement, or a generic call to action.

## Style principles

Prefer:

- concrete workplace language over abstract media framing;
- the current post's own phrases, labels, jokes, and rough names;
- titles that sound like real CNG post titles, not newsletter growth hacks;
- subtitles that explain the consequence or practical use without sounding motivational;
- promo texts that sound like Anatoly introducing the post to colleagues, not a brand promoting content.

Avoid:

- clickbait;
- generic professional media style;
- SEO phrasing;
- consultant language;
- excessive drama;
- motivational promises;
- invented details, scenes, or claims not in the current post;
- carrying over context from any other text.

## Read the post silently

Before generating, silently extract from the current post only:

1. The central model / problem / trap.
2. The strongest original phrases.
3. The practical consequence for the reader.
4. The personal angle: where the author got burned, learned something, changed behavior, or admits uncertainty.
5. The likely target reader.

Do not print this analysis.

## Title rules

Generate exactly **10 title variants**.

Titles should be short enough to be usable as post titles, but not artificially tiny. They may be:

- direct statements;
- questions;
- warnings;
- personal-story titles;
- concept names from the current post;
- rougher CNG-style titles if the current post supports them.

Across 10 variants, include a mix of:

- direct/concept titles;
- practical/warning titles;
- question titles;
- personal-angle titles;
- rougher CNG-style titles if supported by the post.

Do not make every title symmetrical or polished. Minor unevenness is fine if it sounds alive and true to the current post.

## Subtitle rules

Generate exactly **10 subtitle variants**.

Subtitles should complement titles, but they do **not** need to be paired one-to-one. They should be independently useful options.

A CNG-style subtitle usually does one of these:

- states the consequence;
- names the practical use;
- admits the personal source;
- adds a second thought;
- points to a risk, trap, or useful distinction.

Length: usually one sentence, sometimes two short sentences. Keep it specific and useful.

## Short promo text rules

Generate exactly **10 short promo text variants**.

These are for social media / Telegram / Substack notes. Each variant should be **2–5 short sentences** or **one short paragraph**. Use first person when it fits. Promo text should make people understand why to open the post, without pretending it will solve their life.

Across 10 variants, cover different reader states:

- several variants for readers currently inside the problem;
- several variants for readers who went through it in the past;
- several bridge variants that work for both groups;
- at least one variant that leans more personal-authorial;
- at least one variant that leans more system/model/explanation.

Promo texts should:

- use concrete phrases from the current post;
- sound like a human note, not an ad;
- include a hook and a reason to read;
- preserve the author's own uncertainty, roughness, or personal angle when present;
- avoid hashtags unless the user asks;
- avoid emoji unless the user asks;
- avoid generic CTA language.
